"Waking Up in a Programmed World" Free Guy as a Modern Allegory of the Cave

How an NPC's awakening mirrors Plato's warning about illusion, control, and the courage to see the truth.

By Afnan Abdul Lateef | May 31, 2026

Don't you ever feel something’s… off? My friend asked me these questions while we were walking our usual route. I laughed, and yet, these questions stayed hanging in the air long after they were said. It was one of those small statements, which seemed to hold a much bigger meaning.

Most people have probably felt that way at least once. Maybe it happens during a quiet moment in the middle of the day, when everything suddenly feels repetitive. You wake up, check your phone, go to class, eat, scroll, sleep, and then do it all again the next day. Usually we move through those routines without thinking much about them. But every once in a while something feels strange, and you start wondering how much of your life is intentional and how much of it is just habit. It can be uncomfortable to think about, because it makes you question how much control you actually have over your own reality.

That feeling—that maybe there is more going on beneath the surface—is what makes Free Guy so interesting. Under all the humor and action, the movie is really about someone waking up inside a world he was never meant to question. And the more Guy begins to notice about his world, the more the movie starts to feel like a modern version of Plato’s Allegory of the Cave.

Guy in the movie Free Guy is shown as an extremely happy NPC, living his life in a loop, where every day is identical to another. The city of Free City, in which the story takes place, is filled with excitement, adventures, robberies and explosions; however, all of it is just a programmed routine controlled by the developers who make Guy's life predictable.

Living in the Loop

Plato, in his turn, imagines a world, in which the prisoners are living in the illusion created just by their creators. Moreover, in both cases, the illusion breaks only when somebody begins to understand the rules, which he was forced to follow. Thus, although Guy’s world appears bright and full of possibility, it works in the same way as Plato's cave. The daily loops of Guy with all the routines make him believe that everything in this game is ordinary and natural. This is shown most clearly in Guy’s morning routine. Every day he wakes up at the same time, puts on the same blue shirt, greets the goldfish in his apartment, and orders the same coffee with “one sugar.” The repetition feels harmless and even comforting, but that is exactly what makes it meaningful. His routine creates the illusion of stability while revealing how programmed his life actually is.

To put it simply, the prisoners in Plato's allegory believe the shadows that are cast by the firelight to be reality. Similarly, Guy sees the world he lives in as being reality just because that's all he knows. The rules that exist in this virtual game, the behavior of NPCs and many other elements that make up the universe that Guy inhabits become so familiar and so natural for him that he feels free. However, this perception changes when something in the pattern gets disturbed.

The trigger of Guy's awakening happens to be very simple. That awakening becomes even more visible when Guy puts on the sunglasses for the first time. Through them, he suddenly sees the hidden structure of Free City: player statistics, health bars, missions, and objects floating in the environment that were previously invisible to him. The city itself has not changed, but his ability to see it has. This moment closely mirrors the prisoner leaving Plato’s cave and encountering reality beyond the shadows. What was once invisible becomes impossible to ignore once Guy gains a new perspective. Upon noticing Molotov Girl, he gets the first emotion that was not included in his reality thus far, and this triggers his desire to explore things further. This is the point where the questions arise, which inevitably lead Guy to reconsidering whether what he thinks he understands is true.

The Moment of Awakening

Plato says that when one prisoner is freed and brought into the light, the experience is painful. The light hurts his eyes. He doesn’t understand what he is seeing, and at first he wants to go back to the cave because the darkness feels familiar. Guy’s experience feels very similar. Finding out the truth about himself isn't exactly thrilling at first - it's more confusing and a lot to take in. When he discovers he's just a character in a game, that his whole world is made up of code, and that his choices might not be entirely his own, it's like everything he thought he knew gets turned upside down. He's forced to confront all the things he used to believe in, and it's tough. But instead of going back to what's familiar and comfortable, he decides to keep pushing forward, to keep searching for the truth, no matter how hard it gets.

What makes Free Guy feel so relatable is how its world mirrors our own. Even though the story unfolds inside a video game, the concept feels surprisingly familiar. Let's face it, most of us go through our daily routines on autopilot, without giving them much thought. We mindlessly check our favorite apps, open the same old notifications, scroll through our feeds, and consume whatever content comes our way. It all feels pretty normal, because, well, it's just become a part of our daily lives. We're so used to it that we don't even think twice about it. But when you stop and think about it, it's kind of weird how much of our lives are spent stuck in these routines, isn't it?

From Cave to Algorithm: Modern Enslavement

Algorithms are kind of like the people who built Free City - they decide what we see and what grabs our attention. A psychiatrist named Arash Javanbakht calls this the digital tribe, where we're surrounded by stuff that's familiar and reinforces our thoughts. This is pretty much what Guy goes through in Free City, where everything is designed to keep him in a routine. It's like that online too - we move through digital spaces that feel comfy and personalized, but we don't always realize how much work goes into making them that way. These spaces are curated to keep us engaged, and it's not always easy to tell what's real and what's not. Just like Guy, we can get stuck in our own little worlds, without even realizing it. The people who design these digital spaces are like the architects of Free City - they shape our experiences and influence what we see and do. And just as Guy has to navigate the rules of Free City, we have to navigate the algorithms that shape our online lives.

Noam Chomsky takes this idea a step further, saying that the media doesn't just give us information, it actually changes how we see the world. It influences what we pay attention to, what we think is important, and what we consider normal. This is similar to what happens in Plato's cave, where prisoners think the shadows they see are reality because they can't see anything else. Today, people often use digital technology without realizing how it's shaping their thoughts, behaviors, and beliefs. We need to be aware of how these systems are guiding us, and not just accept things at face value. By understanding how the media and digital technology influence us, we can start to see the world in a different way, and make more informed decisions about what we believe and how we live our lives.

It is clear then that such a practice effectively turns the current digital landscape into an advanced iteration of Plato's Cave. Here, rather than hidden puppeteers, one has to refer to Big Tech organizations and their respective coders as those manipulating the reality in a way that will allow the digital cave dwellers to be manipulated in turn. Predictive coding used by developers allows them to create an entire shadow-like reality based entirely on our preferences, thus turning our news feeds and advertisements into a modern iteration of the shadows created by those puppets.

We often think we’re fully in control when we’re online, choosing what to watch, what to click on, or what to pay attention to. But a lot of those choices are shaped before we even make them. Algorithms are constantly predicting what we’ll engage with and placing it in front of us, whether it’s videos, ads, or posts on our feeds. Like Guy in Free Guy, we move through a digital environment designed around patterns we rarely notice. It feels like freedom, but much of what we see has already been selected for us.

This is what makes the digital world feel like a modern version of Plato’s Cave. Just as the prisoners mistake shadows for reality because it’s all they’ve ever known, we can mistake curated content for truth because it fills so much of our daily lives. Platforms like YouTube, TikTok, and Instagram don’t just reflect our interests—they shape them. The more we interact with what is shown to us, the more the system learns, and the more narrowly our reality can become defined by algorithms. Plato’s allegory reminds us that freedom begins when we question what we’re seeing instead of accepting it without thought.

On the other hand, breaking out of this cave proves to be a socially painful endeavor both for the characters from the movie and ourselves. As mentioned earlier, the man who was taken from the darkness of a cave into its sunlight experienced considerable pain and was ridiculed when he attempted to free others. Likewise, trying to disconnect oneself from the digital cave and point out the truth of digital manipulation results in one being socially alienated from the digital tribe. Staying inside the digital cave remains much more comfortable than waking up to its harsh reality.

Breaking Free from the Cave

Such is the case with Free Guy, the very nature of which is centered on the issue of awakening and realization of what really goes on in a digitally manipulated environment. It is important that the main character realizes that something strange is going on only after he notices the pattern surrounding him. This shows that Plato's story is still relatable today. It shows us that freedom isn't just about being given the truth. It's about being curious as that is human nature and asking questions, even if it's uncomfortable. When we stop taking things at face value and start wondering, that's when we start to break free. The first step, as both Guy and Plato's prisoner discover, is simply asking the question: what if there's more to this?

That question my friend asked has really stuck with me.

“Don’t you ever feel like something’s off?”

Because sometimes that’s all it takes. One strange thought. One moment that feels out of place. One question that interrupts the routine. It's one of those things that, once you're aware of it, you can't help but keep noticing.
